Cybersecurity and resiliency aren't just about compliance

Welcome

Today we're going to talk about something that is really critical for all of us in leadership positions – understanding that cybersecurity and resiliency isn't just about ticking boxes on a compliance checklist. It's about making smart, strategic decisions that actually protect our organizations.

Cybersecurity Hierarchy

Cybersecurity (and Resiliency) is fundamentally about managing and accepting risks. Accountability is layered across leadership. At the top, accountability is broad and strategic. It sits with political and senior leadership for public trust and overall posture. As we move down the organizational hierarchy, responsibility becomes more specific.

  • At the Director-level (business owners): responsible for the risks and outcomes of their digital systems and supporting operations.

The CIA Triad

While responsibility is structured hierarchically across the Government, cybersecurity decision making primarily revolves around three areas: Confidentiality, Integrity and Availability, also known as the CIA Triad.

  • Confidentiality means keeping sensitive information private – only authorized people should access it.
  • Integrity means ensuring that data hasn't been tampered with – what you see is accurate and trustworthy.
  • Availability means systems and data are accessible when you need them – they are resilient to downtime.

Resilience Goes Beyond Cybersecurity

We cannot prevent all disruptions, and there is a high cost to ensuring highly available systems. Resiliency goes beyond implementing cybersecurity controls. The focus is to ensure services can continue even when things go wrong. It includes business continuity planning, disaster recovery, and building systems that can recover quickly from disruptions.

Think of it this way: cybersecurity keeps the bad guys out, but resilience ensures you can keep operating as an organisation even if they get in.

Global Cybersecurity Investment

The numbers tell an interesting story. Global spending on cybersecurity products and services have been on an uptrend. But here's the question we need to ask: is all that spending actually making us more secure? Or are we just buying more tools without fundamentally changing our approach?

Rising Threat Landscape

Unfortunately, even with all that spending, breaches are growing exponentially.

Adversaries Are Evolving

The adversaries are getting better, more organized, and more brazen. They have access to increasingly sophisticated tools and technologies, including AI and automation, that make attacks more efficient and scalable. We are up against well-funded, highly skilled professionals.

Singapore: A Prime Target

Singapore is particularly attractive to cybercriminals. Why? Because we're a wealthy nation with advanced digital infrastructure, we're a major financial hub, and we have significant intellectual property across various sectors.

Singapore Resiliency Disruption Timeline

While threat actors have been actively targeting Singapore and our Government, we must also recognise that many service disruptions stem from internal factors like human errors, misconfigurations, and inadequate planning. These are some notable service disruptions in the past 2 years that were not caused by cybersecurity incidents, but such disruptions still have significant impact on our citizens.

Framework for Today's Discussion

Here's how we're going to structure our discussion. We'll categorize cyber threats into three buckets: Preventable, Largely Preventable, and Difficult to Prevent. For each category, we'll look at examples, understand what went wrong, and discuss what we can do about it. Then we'll talk about three critical mindset shifts.

By the end, you should have a practical framework for thinking about cybersecurity that goes beyond compliance checklists.

Chapter 1: Preventable Threats

Basic Hygiene & DDoS Protection

Preventable: Basic Hygiene

Many cyber incidents are preventable with basic security hygiene. This includes DDoS attacks and disruptions caused by inadequate planning and misconfiguration.

The key word here is "preventable." These threats can be effectively mitigated with existing tools, proper planning, and good system design. We know how to stop them — we just need to actually do it.

DDoS: When Servers Are Overwhelmed

Think about your server — which is just a computer — as having a finite capacity to handle requests. Under normal circumstances, a server can handle a few thousand users at a time.

But when millions of requests hit simultaneously, the server simply cannot keep up. It slows down dramatically or crashes entirely. That's a DDoS attack — an attacker sends an overwhelming number of requests, and your services become unavailable.

Network Layer DDoS: Connection Floods

Attackers use botnets — networks of compromised computers and devices — to flood your servers with network connections. When your network resources are exhausted, your servers go down.

Legitimate users can't get through because the botnet has consumed all available connection capacity. This is a classic network layer attack.

Application Layer DDoS: Transactions

Attackers can also use botnets to flood servers with transactions — this is an application layer attack. Instead of just opening connections, attackers make the server do work: requesting web pages, submitting forms, running database queries.

The goal is to exhaust your application resources — CPU, memory, database connections. This can be even more effective than network layer attacks because application-level processing requires more resources.

DDoS-as-a-Service

DDoS attacks are becoming increasingly easy to execute because of tools and services like "DDoS-as-a-Service". Some plans are as cheap as 5 euros per month for a basic service. The barrier to entry for attackers is remarkably low.

Beyond DDoS: Human Factors

DDoS isn't the only type of preventable disruption. Disruptions can also be caused by non-cyber related reasons: inadequate planning, misconfigurations, and human errors.

What Can We Do?

The natural question is: what can we do about all of this? There is actually no need for complicated or expensive solutions.

Recommendation #1: DDoS Protection Services

For DDoS attacks, the solution is straightforward: DDoS Protection Services. They're low-cost and very effective against network layer attacks.

The service sits between the attackers and your servers. When attack traffic comes in, it filters and blocks malicious traffic, only allowing legitimate traffic through. These are typically bundled with Content Delivery Networks (CDNs) like Cloudflare and Akamai.

You get two benefits: faster content delivery for your users AND protection against DDoS attacks.

Recommendation #2: Firewalls

Firewalls are also low-cost and very effective. Positioned right in front of your servers, they provide added protection in case a legitimate-looking request bypasses the DDoS protection service. Firewalls today are quite necessary and not optional add-ons.

Takeaway #1

DDoS and most disruptions are preventable!

  • Use DDoS Protection Services for public-facing systems.
  • Plan for disruption, test for assurance, and implement processes to avoid human errors.

Chapter 2: Largely Preventable

Cybercriminals, Ransomware & Vendor Risk

Largely Preventable Threats

Now we move into the gray zone: largely preventable threats. These are more sophisticated than basic hygiene failures, but they're still preventable with reasonable effort and investment.

Lower-tier APTs: Cybercriminals

One level up from hacktivists and script kiddies, we have lower-tier APTs — cybercriminals. They're more methodical, typically financially motivated, and their most common approach is to deploy ransomware on vulnerable systems and extort money from victims.

While these attacks are more sophisticated than basic threats, they remain largely preventable.

What Can We Do?

What can we do about largely preventable cyber disruptions?

Recommendation #1: Be Aware of Vendor Risk

When outsourcing, you need to be acutely aware of your exposure. Just because you've outsourced doesn't mean you've outsourced the accountability or risk. When your vendor gets hacked, your data gets compromised, your customers are affected, your reputation takes the hit.

  • Develop risk management plans for when vendors get hacked. What data do they access? What services do they provide? What if they went down for a week?
  • Impose requirements on vendors — annual VAPT, security certifications, incident notification timelines. Build these into contracts from day one.

Recommendation #2: Compliance → Outcomes

Many teams approach cybersecurity like a checklist. Did we implement encryption? Check. Did we enable logging? Check. This is the compliance-based mindset — you're verifying that baseline requirements have been implemented.

But it doesn't tell you if you're actually secure. The outcome-based mindset uses Secure Architecting and VAPT to prevent design flaws and discover vulnerabilities specific to your implementation.

  • Secure Architecting — Think like an attacker when designing. Where are the weak points? What assumptions might be wrong?
  • VAPT — Actively try to break into your own systems before the bad guys do.

Recommendation #3: Business Continuity Planning

Plan for continuity and quick recovery for when things go wrong. Notice: "when," not "if."

Officials were able to pivot to manual processing using hardcopy registers during election polling. When immigration clearance systems went down, staff used iPads as a low-tech alternative. These are examples of non-digital fallback plans — just as good as digital alternatives for resilience.

Ask yourself: What are your essential services? What would happen if main systems went down? Do you have manual processes to fall back on?

Takeaway #2

Cyber hygiene issues and complex systems are largely preventable.

  • Be conscious about vendor risk exposure.
  • Conduct VAPT — test that your system cannot break, don't just tick checkboxes.
  • Design systems to be simple for better security and resiliency.
  • Plan for disruptions — have manual fallback procedures and test them regularly.

Chapter 3: Difficult to Prevent

Advanced Persistent Threats & Zero-Days

Difficult to Prevent

Now we come to the third and last category: difficult to prevent threats.

Advanced Persistent Threats

APTs are typically nation-state actors or highly sophisticated criminal groups. They're called 'persistent' because they don't give up easily — sometimes spending months or years inside a network before achieving their objectives.

These groups have access to zero-day vulnerabilities that software vendors don't know about yet. They use sophisticated social engineering and have resources to develop custom malware. You're not just fighting a hacker — you're fighting an intelligence agency.

Even Mature Organisations Fall Victim

Organizations with mature security programs can fall victim to APT attacks. Microsoft is an example of a highly sophisticated organisation that was successfully targeted.

APT Storm-0558 (Antique Typhoon)

Antique Typhoon is a China-based threat actor. The objective of most of this group's campaigns is to obtain unauthorized access to email accounts for harvesting information and phishing. They targeted Microsoft directly.

What Are Zero-Day Exploits?

Zero-day exploits are vulnerabilities unknown to the software vendor — meaning there's no patch available. How do you defend against something that no one knows exists?

This is where defense-in-depth becomes critical. Even if an attacker exploits a zero-day for initial access, you want multiple layers of defense: network segmentation, application whitelisting, behavior-based detection — these controls help even when signature-based defenses fail.

APTs Are ROI-Sensitive

Purchasing powerful exploits is extremely expensive. So APTs only pursue targets that are worth their resources. This means they are strategic and selective in their targeting.

What APTs Target

APTs usually want two things:

  • Secrets related to defense or security
  • Disruptions by taking over critical infrastructure

What Can We Do?

What can we do about APTs?

Recommendation: Air-Gap What Is Truly Secret

Decide what information is truly secret. Traditionally, organizations with sensitive data use air-gapped networks — physically isolated from the internet.

However, even air-gapped networks can be breached. The Stuxnet attack on Iranian nuclear facilities proved this in 2010 — attackers used a USB drive to bridge the air gap. More recently in 2024, GoldenJackal used custom tools to target an air-gapped embassy in Belarus.

Air-gapping provides protection but isn't foolproof. You still need physical security, strict controls on removable media, and monitoring within the air-gapped environment.

Some Disruptions Are Beyond Our Control

Examples include Microsoft's data center outage and CrowdStrike's buggy update causing global disruption. Even the most prepared organisations can face external failures.

Takeaway #3

APT attacks and outage of cloud services are very difficult to prevent.

  • For secrets, use of air-gapped networks can reduce our exposure to an external attacker.
  • But air-gapped environments are also very costly and low in accessibility, so there is a need to be judicious over the use of such networks.
  • Critical systems need well-planned and rehearsed non-digital Business Continuity Plans (BCPs).

Mindset Shifts

Three Strategic Shifts for Leaders

Three Mindset Shifts

Technical controls aside, we should also embrace three mindset shifts as leaders.

Shift #1: Make Upfront Trade-offs

We need to be honest: security, cost, and usability exist in tension. You cannot maximize all three simultaneously. The key is to consciously decide where you want to be on this spectrum.

The Security-Cost-Usability Trilemma

You have three competing goals: Security/Resilience, Cost Savings, and Usability. You can pick two, but you can't have all three at maximum levels.

Stick to Your Trade-offs

We often start with security in mind. However, as pressures mount on usability, we sometimes loosen measures — resulting in neither great user experience nor security.

We need to understand the risks involved, make trade-offs upfront and stick with them.

This happens when trade-offs were not made consciously, so you second-guess yourself every time someone complains that security is inconvenient. You made the decision for good reasons — stick to it.

🏋️ Exercise: Crown Jewels Trilemma

Take a moment to think about these two questions:

  • What are your organization's 'crown jewels' — your most critical assets, data, and systems? What would hurt most if compromised or unavailable?
  • What support would help you better manage these trade-offs? More budget? Better tools? Executive support?

Shift #2: From Compliance to Outcomes

Many organizations treat security as a compliance exercise — implementing controls to satisfy auditors, but not thinking about whether those controls are effective.

We need to shift from asking 'Are we compliant?' to asking 'Are we actually secure?'

The Limitations of Compliance

Compliance frameworks have value — they provide a baseline. But relying on traditional audit compliance is insufficient for cybersecurity.

Why? There is no one-size-fits-all standard. Systems vary greatly in setup and risk levels. More controls ≠ more security. You can implement dozens of controls poorly and be less secure than an organisation with a few controls implemented well.

Move to Risk-Based Approach

Three steps to make this shift:

  1. Understand the risk — What data or services does the system handle? What's the potential impact?
  2. Design mitigations — Based on risk, what controls are appropriate? Don't just apply a standard template.
  3. Outcomes-based testing — Don't just check if controls are configured — test if they actually work. Can your monitoring detect an attack? Would your backup restore successfully?

Surface Vulnerabilities Proactively

As we conduct more security testing, more vulnerabilities will be uncovered. That might sound bad, but it's actually good. It's much better to find vulnerabilities during testing than to have attackers find them in production.

We should encourage teams to surface vulnerabilities proactively for better visibility, rather than hiding them for fear of looking bad. Security is a team sport, not a blame game.

Shift #3: Assume Breach. Assume Fail.

The third mindset shift: Assume Breach, Assume Fail. This is about acknowledging that there is no perfect resilience. No matter how good your security is, plan for the possibility of both a successful breach and the failure of your resilience measures.

SOC vs. Threat Hunting

A traditional Security Operations Center is like castle guards — they rely on known indicators. If the attacker uses a new technique, they might slip past.

Threat hunting is different — hunters proactively search based on understanding of attacker behaviour. They develop hypotheses on potential risks and actively validate them. Both are needed, but threat hunting represents the outcomes mindset.

Red Teams

Red teams help organisations test their assumptions. GovTech's red team has partnered with many mature agencies to uncover overlooked vulnerabilities.

Business Continuity Plans

Critical systems need well-planned and rehearsed non-digital BCPs. If you have a digital BCP stored on the very systems that just failed, how are you going to access it?

You need printed copies, offline documentation, and procedures that work without IT systems. And they need to be rehearsed — beyond tabletop exercises.

The Restaurant Analogy

Resilience is like keeping a restaurant running:

  • Prevent fires — fire-safe equipment, proper electrical systems, training. Like your cybersecurity controls.
  • Mitigate impact — fire alarms, sprinklers, insurance. Like detection and response capabilities.
  • Keep operating — fire exits, emergency procedures, contingency plans. That's business continuity planning.

Things Will Go Wrong

Despite our best efforts, things can and will go wrong. Accepting this reality is the first step to true resilience.

The Cost of Over-Insurance

"Over-insuring" through prevention leads to very high costs. For example, guarding against cloud outages with a multi-cloud approach can 2x to 10x the cost. Having on-premise architecture isn't necessarily more resilient either.

Summary

We covered three categories of threats: Preventable, Largely Preventable, and Difficult to Prevent. We discussed APTs and the challenges of sophisticated attackers with significant resources.

And three critical mindset shifts:

  1. Make upfront trade-offs between security, cost, and usability.
  2. Move from compliance to outcomes.
  3. Assume breach, assume fail.

Perfect security is impossible, but significant improvement is within reach for most of us. Start with the basics, make conscious trade-offs, and focus on outcomes.
